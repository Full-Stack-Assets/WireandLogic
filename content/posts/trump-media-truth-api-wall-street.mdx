Trump Media & Technology Group (TMTG) announced on July 16 that it will launch a paid data feed called Truth API, delivering posts from the platform’s most influential accounts in milliseconds. The service, slated to go live on August 1, targets Wall Street firms that need ultra‑low‑latency information to feed algorithmic trading strategies. By streaming updates from the top ten ranking accounts—including President Donald Trump—around the clock, the feed turns political chatter into a market data product. For a loss‑making media company, the move promises a new high‑margin revenue stream, while traders gain a legal alternative to the scraping methods they’ve used for months.

<Callout type="takeaway">Truth API turns political chatter into a high‑speed data product for traders.</Callout>

## What happened
TMTG said the Truth API will deliver posts from the ten most influential Truth Social accounts in "milliseconds" and will operate 24/7, with an archive dating back to 2022. The company described the feed as a licensed, real‑time service designed for organizations most impacted by the cost of a delay, such as high‑frequency trading firms.

Previously, banks and traders had to monitor the app manually or scrape data in violation of TMTG’s terms of service. The new feed replaces those ad‑hoc methods with an official channel, and the company has already signed up customers ahead of the August 1 launch.

Interim CEO Kevin McGurn said the offering will become a "meaningful, ongoing source of revenue" for TMTG, though the firm did not disclose pricing. The service is expected to create a steady new source of money for the loss‑making company.

## Why it matters
For traders, a delay of even seconds can translate into massive profit or loss, so millisecond‑level access to market‑moving signals offers a clear competitive edge. The feed also formalizes a data‑licensing model that many social platforms already use, potentially reshaping how political statements become tradable information.

However, the product blurs the line between a private business and the President’s public communications, raising conflict‑of‑interest and regulatory concerns. Regulators may scrutinize whether privileged access to presidential posts creates an unfair advantage or violates insider‑trading rules.

## How to think about it
Developers building trading systems should treat the Truth API like any other market data feed: evaluate latency guarantees, implement robust authentication, and monitor data quality. Incorporate compliance checks to ensure that using politically sourced information does not breach securities regulations. Finally, factor the subscription cost—once disclosed—into the overall economics of any latency‑driven strategy.

<Callout type="warning">Do not assume that faster access to political posts is exempt from insider‑trading laws; ensure your usage complies with SEC and other relevant regulations.</Callout>
