The European Union's proposed age verification application has sparked concerns over its reliance on Google and Apple technologies. The app, intended to verify age in a privacy-preserving manner, requires users to have either a Google or Apple account and a Google-approved Android device or an iPhone. This has raised concerns about the EU's dependency on US technology companies, particularly at a time when the bloc is trying to reduce its reliance on American tech giants.

<Callout type="takeaway">The EU's age verification app may inadvertently deepen its dependency on American tech giants, contradicting efforts to reduce reliance on US technology.</Callout>

## What happened
The EU's age verification application is currently being tested on a voluntary basis by several member states. The app is open-source, with its code hosted on GitHub. However, the application's requirement for Google Play Integrity API and Apple App Attestation has raised concerns. This means that the app will only work on Google-approved Android devices and iPhones, and users will need to have a Google or Apple account to use it.

## Why it matters
The EU's reliance on Google and Apple technologies for age verification has significant implications. It may limit the use of the app to only those with approved devices and accounts, excluding users with alternative operating systems or devices. This could undermine the EU's efforts to promote digital sovereignty and reduce its dependency on US technology companies.
