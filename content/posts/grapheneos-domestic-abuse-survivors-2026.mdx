## What happened
Tech‑facilitated abuse now appears in 99% of domestic‑violence incidents, ranging from GPS stalking to hidden spyware. Children are also affected, with 27% of cases involving technology‑related harm. Over the past five years, reports of such abuse have risen as much as 650% in some Australian states, highlighting the scale of the problem.

GrapheneOS, running on recent Google Pixel hardware, offers several defenses tailored for high‑risk users. Features include app isolation with up to 32 hidden profiles, Verified Boot that detects tampering, and a duress PIN that can instantly wipe encrypted data. The OS strips out unnecessary telemetry and provides physical kill switches, dramatically reducing the data surface an abuser could exploit.

## Why it matters
When standard smartphones continuously share location, app usage, and sensor data, abusers can covertly monitor survivors. Reducing that data flow can mean the difference between safety and ongoing control. By adopting a de‑Googled OS with strong hardware-backed security, survivors gain practical privacy without sacrificing essential communication tools. This shift also pressures mainstream manufacturers to consider privacy‑first designs.

## How to think about it
Start by selecting a recent Pixel model that receives long‑term updates. Back up all data before unlocking the bootloader, then follow the official GrapheneOS flashing guide. After installation, create a hidden profile for everyday use and reserve the primary profile for emergency contacts only. Enable the duress PIN and test the wipe function in a safe environment. Finally, install only vetted apps, preferably from reputable sources, and consider a lightweight Play Store or microG for essential services.

<Callout type="warning">Do not install GrapheneOS on a phone without first backing up data; unlocking the bootloader will erase everything and may void the device warranty.</Callout>
