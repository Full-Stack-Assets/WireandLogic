Microsoft's Xbox division has reportedly initiated significant layoffs, impacting numerous studios including id Software. Sources indicate that approximately half of id Software's staff, critically including many members of the id Tech engine team, have been let go. This move has sparked considerable concern within the gaming community and among developers, raising questions about the future of id Software's renowned franchises like DOOM and the long-term strategy for its powerful id Tech engine. The cuts come amidst a broader restructuring at Xbox, aiming to consolidate around "strongest franchises" but leaving many veteran developers displaced.

<Callout type="takeaway">
Microsoft's recent layoffs at id Software, particularly within the id Tech team, signal a potential shift away from proprietary engine development within Xbox's strategy, impacting the future of its iconic FPS franchises.
</Callout>

## What happened
Reports indicate that around half of id Software's staff, including "most, if not all," of its coders, were reportedly laid off. This includes long-serving veterans such as Michael Maynard, who dedicated 21 years to id Software, contributing significantly to the evolution of the id Tech engine. These reductions are part of a larger wave of layoffs across Xbox's studios, with thousands of employees expected to be affected throughout FY2027 as Microsoft undertakes a significant restructuring effort.

Beyond id Software, other prominent studios under the Xbox umbrella, such as Obsidian Entertainment, also experienced substantial cuts, with an estimated 60-70 developers impacted across various roles. While the stated goal of this restructuring is for Bethesda to focus on its "strongest franchises" like Fallout, The Elder Scrolls, Doom, Quake, and Wolfenstein, the scale of id Software's cuts, especially to its core technology team, has led to confusion and concern about how these flagship titles will be supported and innovated upon going forward.

## Why it matters
The significant reduction in id Software's engineering talent, particularly those responsible for the id Tech engine, casts a considerable shadow over the future development and innovation potential of this renowned technology. id Tech has historically served as a benchmark for first-person shooter technology, consistently pushing boundaries in graphics and performance. A diminished team could hinder its ability to compete and evolve against widely adopted commercial engines like Unreal Engine, potentially leading to a stagnation in its unique capabilities.

These layoffs reflect a broader industry trend of consolidation and cost-cutting, where large publishers may increasingly favor standardized, externally licensed engines over maintaining expensive, bespoke internal technology teams. While focusing on "strongest franchises" sounds strategically sound, gutting the core tech team of one of those foundational franchises suggests a fundamental shift in how Xbox views internal engine development versus external solutions. This could contribute to a more homogenized gaming landscape if fewer proprietary engines are developed and maintained across the industry.

## How to think about it
Developers should view these events as a stark reminder of the evolving landscape in large game development studios. The emphasis on "strongest franchises" coupled with cuts to core technology teams suggests a pivot towards leveraging existing, proven engines or external solutions rather than investing heavily in proprietary tech for every studio. For those working on specialized engines, this highlights the importance of demonstrating clear, quantifiable value and adaptability within a larger corporate structure. For individual developers, particularly those in engine-focused roles, diversifying skill sets to include widely adopted commercial engines (like Unreal or Unity) alongside specialized knowledge can offer greater career resilience. The industry appears to be moving towards a model where unique technological advantages might be centralized or outsourced, rather than maintained across numerous internal teams.

<Callout type="warning">
Developers relying solely on highly specialized, proprietary engine knowledge within a single large studio should consider expanding their expertise to more widely adopted commercial engines to mitigate career risk in an increasingly consolidating industry.
</Callout>
