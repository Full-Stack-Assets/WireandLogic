Michigan is considering a bill to restrict employers from requiring after-hours communication. This legislative effort, known as the Workplace Employee Boundaries Act (Senate Bill 948), aims to establish clearer boundaries between work and personal life. If passed, it would significantly impact how employers and employees interact outside of standard work hours, addressing the growing expectation of constant availability in the modern economy. The bill seeks to protect employee well-being and family life by limiting demands for off-the-clock responses to work-related messages.

<Callout type="takeaway">The Michigan Workplace Employee Boundaries Act proposes to mandate a "right to disconnect" for employees, preventing employers from requiring responses to work communications outside of assigned hours.</Callout>

## What happened
Michigan's Senate Bill 948, dubbed the Workplace Employee Boundaries Act, has been introduced by Senator Erika Geiss and referred to the Labor Committee. The proposed legislation seeks to regulate when and why employers can contact employees outside of their scheduled work hours. Its primary goal is to address the pervasive "always-on" culture that often blurs the lines between professional and personal time.

Under the bill, employers would generally be prohibited from requiring employees to access or respond to work-related matters, including emails, text messages, or social media messages concerning duties or scheduling, outside of their assigned hours. Exceptions include state or federal emergencies affecting business operations. The bill also outlines provisions for employees to be compensated for on-call availability or to set specific hours for responding to work matters, if agreed upon in their contract.

Violations of the Workplace Employee Boundaries Act could be reported to the state's Department of Labor and Economic Opportunity. Potential repercussions for companies include fines and/or requirements to pay overtime to affected employees. The administrative burden on the Department of Labor and Economic Opportunity to develop training materials and process complaints is noted as a potential cost.

## Why it matters
This bill reflects a growing global trend to legislate the "right to disconnect," acknowledging the detrimental effects of perpetual work availability on employee mental health, family life, and overall well-being. For developers and builders, who often face project deadlines and collaborative demands that extend beyond typical office hours, such legislation could provide much-needed clarity and protection against burnout. It shifts the burden of managing work-life boundaries from the individual employee to a legal framework, potentially fostering a healthier work culture across industries. The implications extend to how companies structure remote work, on-call rotations, and communication policies, requiring a re-evaluation of current practices to ensure compliance and respect for employee off-hours.

## How to think about it
For organizations operating in or considering Michigan, this bill underscores the importance of proactive policy development. Rather than viewing it as a restriction, consider it an opportunity to optimize communication strategies and foster a culture of respect for employee time. Implement clear guidelines for after-hours communication, define what constitutes an emergency, and explore tools that facilitate asynchronous collaboration. For employees, understanding your rights under such legislation empowers you to advocate for healthier boundaries. This isn't about avoiding work, but about ensuring that work is conducted within reasonable and agreed-upon parameters, ultimately leading to more sustainable productivity and employee satisfaction.

<Callout type="warning">Employers should carefully review their contracts for on-call employees and emergency communication protocols to ensure compliance, as non-adherence could result in fines and overtime payment obligations.</Callout>
