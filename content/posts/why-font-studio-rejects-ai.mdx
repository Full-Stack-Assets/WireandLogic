Amid a surge of generative‑AI tools, a boutique type‑design studio announced it will not incorporate any AI into its workflow. The statement, posted on its website and echoed in a Hacker News thread, outlines a deliberate choice to keep font creation fully human. The studio argues that the tactile friction of hand‑crafting letters is essential to the evolution of type. As AI services become cheaper, the decision raises questions about the future of creative labor in design.

<Callout type="takeaway">Human‑driven type design preserves the cultural lineage that AI cannot replicate.</Callout>

## What happened
The studio published an essay titled “From human hands” that traces the millennia‑long evolution of letterforms, from prehistoric carvings to Roman serifs, emphasizing how each stroke reflects the maker’s physical constraints. It argues that delegating design to generative models removes the very friction that historically drives typographic innovation, and notes that its own website briefly crashed under a traffic spike after the article went viral.

In a related Hacker News discussion, the author clarified that the stance is not an outright rejection of AI but a refusal to use it for core font design tasks. The thread highlighted mixed community sentiment: some praise the ethical stand against data‑theft and loss of craft, while others point to AI’s potential for rapid prototyping and accessibility.

## Why it matters
The debate pits cultural heritage against efficiency. Fonts are visual vocabularies that shape reading experiences worldwide; altering their creation process could dilute the historical dialogue embedded in each glyph. At the same time, studios that ignore AI risk falling behind on cost and speed, potentially limiting their market reach. The decision therefore signals a broader tension between preserving artisanal value and embracing automation in creative industries.

## How to think about it
When evaluating AI for design, start by categorizing tasks: repetitive layout, copy generation, or exploratory sketches can benefit from automation, while core creative decisions that define a typeface’s character should remain human‑driven. Establish clear guidelines that define where AI assistance ends and craftsmanship begins. Regularly audit the data sources feeding any model to avoid inadvertent copyright infringement. Finally, communicate your policy transparently to clients and collaborators to set expectations.

<Callout type="warning">Do not assume that all AI tools are harmful; indiscriminate rejection can forfeit legitimate productivity gains and hinder experimentation.</Callout>
