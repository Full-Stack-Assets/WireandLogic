Across the US, Europe, and Australia, new age verification regulations are being introduced, often framed as essential measures to protect children online. However, a growing number of observers argue that these laws serve a far more insidious purpose: to establish a pervasive system for the automated attribution of online speech to real-world identities. This shift represents a significant erosion of digital anonymity and could fundamentally alter the landscape of free expression and privacy for internet users globally.

<Callout type="takeaway">
Age verification laws are not merely about protecting minors, but are foundational steps toward systems that automatically link online actions and speech to an individual's physical identity.
</Callout>

## What happened
Recent legislative efforts in numerous jurisdictions, including several US states, European nations, and Australia, have mandated age verification for accessing various online platforms and content. These regulations are publicly presented with the goal of safeguarding minors from harmful material, a talking point often invoked to garner broad public support. Proponents emphasize the need for robust mechanisms to prevent underage access to adult-oriented content and services.

However, a deeper analysis reveals that these laws inherently function as identity attribution systems. By requiring users to link their digital accounts to official government-issued identification or other personal data, these systems create a direct, undeniable connection between an online persona and a physical identity. This process moves beyond traditional investigative methods, which often involve significant human effort and legal hurdles to uncover real identities behind pseudonyms.

## Why it matters
The implications of widespread identity attribution are profound, particularly for free speech and privacy. Historically, law enforcement and other authorities have faced challenges in identifying individuals behind online activities, often relying on time-consuming OSINT or subpoenas. Age verification systems streamline this process, potentially enabling rapid, even automated, identification of individuals based on their online posts or interactions, regardless of whether a crime has been committed.

This shift empowers authorities to bypass traditional investigative safeguards, creating a pathway for monitoring and potentially penalizing inconvenient or dissenting voices. The ability to quickly tie any online statement to a real person could have a chilling effect on public discourse, discouraging critical commentary or activism. For developers and builders, this means navigating an environment where user anonymity is increasingly compromised, impacting platform design, data handling, and user trust.

## How to think about it
Developers and users alike should approach age verification mandates with a critical perspective, recognizing their potential dual-use nature. For individuals, exercising caution regarding identity verification requests is paramount. If forced to comply, exploring privacy-preserving alternatives, such as services that offer anonymous payment options like Monero for verification, can mitigate some risks. For platform builders, this trend highlights the importance of designing systems with privacy by design, advocating for robust user data protection, and understanding the legal landscape of identity attribution. It's crucial to consider the long-term societal impact of integrating such systems and to actively push back against overreaching surveillance capabilities.

<Callout type="warning">
Be extremely wary of any system demanding your real-world identity for online access; these are often designed to enable broad surveillance and attribution of your digital activities, potentially leading to unforeseen consequences for your privacy and freedom of speech. Avoid verifying your age if possible, or seek privacy-centric verification methods.
</Callout>
