## What happened
Iran's Persian Gulf Strait Authority has announced that ships must have mandatory insurance to transit the Strait of Hormuz. The insurance policy is currently free, but the authority has reserved the right to introduce fees in the future. Ships must follow a prescribed route that passes along Iran's coast, and alternatives are prohibited. The US has said that more than 20 vessels had traveled through the waterway overnight, implying they did so with their signals off.

The shipping industry is trying to assess the safety of transiting the strait after the interim peace deal was reached. Western naval groups have recommended a corridor along Oman's waters as the main transit route, which could lead to the opening of parallel shipping lanes.

Iran's move to impose mandatory insurance is seen as an attempt to assert control over the strategic waterway. The US and its allies have warned that Iran's attempts to introduce fees to pass the strait would break with international maritime law and set a dangerous precedent.

## Why it matters
The Strait of Hormuz is a critical waterway for the global energy trade, with a significant portion of the world's oil passing through it. Any disruption to shipping in the strait could have significant economic and geopolitical implications. The imposition of fees by Iran could lead to increased costs for shippers and oil producers, which could be passed on to consumers. The move also raises concerns about the safety of transiting the strait, particularly given the presence of mines in the area.
