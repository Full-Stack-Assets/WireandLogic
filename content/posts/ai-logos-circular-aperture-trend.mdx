If you’ve been scrolling through AI startup websites lately, you’ve probably noticed a strange visual similarity. Most of the newest AI company logos consist of a clean circle, a subtle gradient, and a hollow center that draws the eye inward. The pattern has been pointed out by observers since at least 2023, when a mainstream design article described it as the “swirling hexagon” trend. Designers and marketers argue the shape signals openness, precision, and a futuristic aesthetic, while critics see it as a bland, almost anatomical metaphor. Understanding why the motif spreads helps founders decide whether to follow or reject it.

<Callout type="takeaway">Circular, hollow logos have become the default visual shorthand for AI branding.</Callout>

## What happened
The phenomenon was first highlighted in a 2023 FastCompany piece that avoided anatomical language and labeled the style “the swirling hexagon.” Subsequent commentary on sites like VelvetShark and TechTonic Shifts repeated the observation, noting that OpenAI’s recent redesign features a perfect circle with a subtle gradient and a central void. Anthropic’s Claude logo pushes the motif further, while DeepSeek and Midjourney break the pattern with sea‑related, non‑circular symbols.

Design teams often justify the look with language about “circles representing the dynamic intersection between humanity and technology” and “right angles introducing precision.” The same visual grammar appears in Android system icons and other consumer‑tech branding, suggesting a broader industry convergence around circular mandalas that focus attention on a central point.

## Why it matters
When many AI firms share a near‑identical visual language, brand differentiation becomes harder, and investors may struggle to recall a specific company based on its logo alone. The hollow‑center motif also subtly reinforces the perception of AI as a “black box” – an opaque system that draws users in without revealing its inner workings. On the other hand, the consistency signals a shared cultural understanding of what AI looks like, which can accelerate user trust for newcomers who recognize the pattern.

## How to think about it
Treat the circular motif as a design vocabulary, not a prescription. Start by asking what emotional tone your brand needs – openness, precision, mystery – and map those to shape, color, and texture. If you choose a circle, differentiate with unique gradients, motion, or secondary graphic elements that break the monotony. Consider alternative geometries (hexagons, polygons, organic shapes) if you want to signal divergence from the mainstream. Test mock‑ups with real users to gauge whether the logo feels fresh or merely derivative before committing to a final mark.

<Callout type="warning">Avoid adopting the trend solely for hype; a logo that feels copied can damage credibility and may lead to trademark conflicts.</Callout>
