The European Union has taken a significant step towards reducing electronic waste by introducing new regulations that require smartphones to have removable batteries by 2027. This move aims to increase device lifespan and reduce the environmental impact of discarded phones. The regulation applies to all smartphones sold in the EU, with some exceptions for devices that meet specific safety and durability standards. <Callout type="takeaway">The EU's new regulation will make it easier for consumers to replace their phone batteries, reducing electronic waste and promoting sustainability.</Callout> ## What happened The EU has been working on this regulation for several years, with the goal of reducing electronic waste and promoting sustainability. The new rule requires phone manufacturers to design devices with readily removable and replaceable batteries, without the need for specialized tools. This means that consumers will be able to replace their phone batteries easily, reducing the need for new devices and minimizing waste. The regulation also applies to other portable devices, such as tablets and smart glasses. According to the EU, around 150 million smartphones and 24 million tablets are sold in the EU each year, resulting in approximately 5 million tonnes of electronic waste. However, less than 40% of this waste is properly recycled. The new regulation aims to change this by making it easier for consumers to repair and replace their devices, rather than discarding them. ## Why it matters The EU's new regulation has significant implications for the environment, consumers, and phone manufacturers. By making it easier to replace phone batteries, the regulation can help reduce electronic waste, decrease the demand for new devices, and promote sustainability. This, in turn, can help reduce the environmental impact of the tech industry, which is a significant contributor to greenhouse gas emissions and waste. The regulation also has the potential to save consumers money, as they will no longer need to purchase new devices when their batteries degrade.
